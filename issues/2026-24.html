Policy & Regulation

European Union — AI Act enforcement bodies formally appointed (June 1) The European Commission appointed the two independent expert bodies that support the AI Office: a Scientific Panel of 60 independent experts (focused on general-purpose AI, systemic risk, model classification, evaluation methodology, and cross-border market surveillance) and a 174-member Advisory Forum drawn from civil society, academia, and industry. Members serve two-year terms. (European Commission · AI Scientific Panel)

🛡️ Why it matters for SDC — The EU now has named institutions, not just statutory text, behind GPAI and systemic-risk enforcement. For clients with EU-facing general-purpose or high-risk systems, “who classifies a model as systemic-risk and on what methodology” now has an address — factor it into AI-governance and model-evaluation roadmaps.

Date cleanup — EU AI Act high-risk timeline (resolving last week’s flagged conflict): Under the Act as adopted, prohibited practices (since Feb 2025) and GPAI obligations (since Aug 2025) are in force; standalone high-risk obligations apply from August 2, 2026, with product-embedded high-risk systems extended to August 2, 2028. The Digital/AI Omnibus postponement of the high-risk dates is provisionally agreed but not yet formally adopted, so August 2, 2026 remains the legal default until that adoption happens. (European Commission — Regulatory framework)

🎯 Why it matters for Accenture — Multinationals should plan to the Act-as-adopted August 2, 2026 high-risk date, not the provisional Omnibus relief. Treating the postponement as if it were law is the kind of assumption that turns into a compliance gap if the Omnibus slips.

Business & Funding

NVIDIA — multiyear memory partnership with SK hynix; gigawatt-scale AI-factory deals with LG and Naver (June 7) NVIDIA and SK hynix announced a multiyear partnership to co-develop next-generation memory for the AI-factory buildout — explicitly aligning SK hynix’s memory supply with NVIDIA’s roadmap (Vera Rubin AI supercomputers, Vera CPUs, RTX Spark PCs, Jetson Thor) and applying NVIDIA’s own stack (CUDA-X, PhysicsNeMo, Omniverse/cuOpt) to chip design and autonomous-fab digital twins. The same day, NVIDIA announced “gigawatt-scale” AI-factory collaborations with LG and Naver in South Korea. (NVIDIA · GlobeNewswire)

🎯 Why it matters for Accenture — This is the same structural signal we flagged in the Anthropic Series H (memory vendors on the cap table): HBM/next-gen memory supply is being pre-committed around NVIDIA’s roadmap. For clients planning AI-infrastructure spend, memory and AI-factory capacity is increasingly allocated through multiyear vendor alignment, not open-market purchase — a procurement-timing and supply-risk conversation, not just a chip-price one.

Moonshot AI (Kimi) — reportedly raising ~$2B at a ~$30B valuation (June 8) Per Bloomberg, the Beijing-based maker of the Kimi chatbot is reportedly in talks to raise more than $1B in a round that would value it at roughly $30 billion — its third raise in six months (a ~$20B round led by Meituan closed in May; the company was valued near $4B in December). (Bloomberg · TechCrunch)

🎯 Why it matters for Accenture — A roughly 7× valuation climb in six months for a Chinese open-weight frontier lab is a market signal worth tracking: clients evaluating open-source model strategies increasingly have a credible non-US frontier option, with the attendant data-residency, export-control, and supply-chain questions that come with it.

Enterprise & Security Implications

Microsoft Semantic Kernel agent-RCE — CVEs assigned and patches shipped (resolves last week’s open watch item) The Semantic Kernel “prompt-injection-to-host-RCE” path flagged last week (via a single security roundup) now has concrete vendor detail. Microsoft assigned two CVEs: CVE-2026-26030, where the In-Memory Vector Store’s default filter builds a Python lambda at runtime and executes it with eval() — turning prompt injection into host-level RCE — and CVE-2026-25592, where the .NET SDK’s SessionsPythonPlugin exposed a host-side file-download method as a callable kernel function the model could invoke as a tool. MSRC advisories and the “When prompts become shells” research post published May 7, 2026; fixes shipped in semantic-kernel ≥1.39.4 (Python) and ≥1.71.0 (.NET), adding an AST allowlist, a function-call allowlist, and a dangerous-attribute blocklist. (Microsoft Security Blog · PointGuard AI)

🛡️ Why it matters for SDC — Two concrete client actions: (1) inventory and upgrade — any client running Semantic Kernel should be on Python ≥1.39.4 / .NET ≥1.71.0; (2) generalize the pattern — any agent framework that constructs and then executes code from model output (eval, dynamic lambdas, runtime-exposed host functions) is RCE-by-design, not by accident. Make “does the framework eval model-influenced strings?” a standard question in agentic-AI security reviews.

Unverified rumor flagged for tracking — A separate “CVE-2026-2256” (reported as a critical MS-Agent prompt-injection-to-command-execution flaw, CVSS 9.8) circulated this week, but the primary write-up was inaccessible and we could not confirm it against NVD or an MSRC advisory. REVIEW: tracking only — do not cite to clients until verified against the CVE record.
