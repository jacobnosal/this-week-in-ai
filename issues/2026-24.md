## Policy & Regulation

**European Union — AI Act enforcement bodies formally appointed (June 1)**
The European Commission appointed the two independent expert bodies that support the AI Office: a **Scientific Panel** of 60 independent experts (focused on general-purpose AI, systemic risk, model classification, evaluation methodology, and cross-border market surveillance) and a **174-member Advisory Forum** drawn from civil society, academia, and industry. Members serve two-year terms. ([European Commission](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/news/ai-act-enforcement-gets-independent-expert-support) · [AI Scientific Panel](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/ai-scientific-panel))

> **🛡️ Why it matters for SDC** — The EU now has named institutions, not just statutory text, behind GPAI and systemic-risk enforcement. For clients with EU-facing general-purpose or high-risk systems, "who classifies a model as systemic-risk and on what methodology" now has an address — factor it into AI-governance and model-evaluation roadmaps.

**Date cleanup — EU AI Act high-risk timeline** (resolving last week's flagged conflict): Under the Act **as adopted**, prohibited practices (since Feb 2025) and GPAI obligations (since Aug 2025) are in force; **standalone high-risk obligations apply from August 2, 2026**, with product-embedded high-risk systems extended to August 2, 2028. The Digital/AI Omnibus postponement of the high-risk dates is **provisionally agreed but not yet formally adopted**, so August 2, 2026 remains the legal default until that adoption happens. ([European Commission — Regulatory framework](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai))

> **🎯 Why it matters for Accenture** — Multinationals should plan to the Act-as-adopted August 2, 2026 high-risk date, not the provisional Omnibus relief. Treating the postponement as if it were law is the kind of assumption that turns into a compliance gap if the Omnibus slips.

## Enterprise & Security Implications

**Microsoft Semantic Kernel agent-RCE — CVEs assigned and patches shipped** (resolves last week's open watch item)
The Semantic Kernel "prompt-injection-to-host-RCE" path flagged last week (via a single security roundup) now has concrete vendor detail. Microsoft assigned two CVEs: **CVE-2026-26030**, where the In-Memory Vector Store's default filter builds a Python lambda at runtime and executes it with `eval()` — turning prompt injection into host-level RCE — and **CVE-2026-25592**, where the .NET SDK's `SessionsPythonPlugin` exposed a host-side file-download method as a callable kernel function the model could invoke as a tool. MSRC advisories and the "When prompts become shells" research post published **May 7, 2026**; fixes shipped in **semantic-kernel ≥1.39.4 (Python)** and **≥1.71.0 (.NET)**, adding an AST allowlist, a function-call allowlist, and a dangerous-attribute blocklist. ([Microsoft Security Blog](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2026/05/07/prompts-become-shells-rce-vulnerabilities-ai-agent-frameworks/) · [PointGuard AI](https://www.pointguardai.com/ai-security-incidents/semantic-kernel-lets-a-prompt-open-a-shell-cve-2026-25592-cve-2026-26030))

> **🛡️ Why it matters for SDC** — Two concrete client actions: (1) **inventory and upgrade** — any client running Semantic Kernel should be on Python ≥1.39.4 / .NET ≥1.71.0; (2) generalize the pattern — **any agent framework that constructs and then executes code from model output** (`eval`, dynamic lambdas, runtime-exposed host functions) is RCE-by-design, not by accident. Make "does the framework eval model-influenced strings?" a standard question in agentic-AI security reviews.

> **Unverified rumor flagged for tracking** — A separate "CVE-2026-2256" (reported as a critical MS-Agent prompt-injection-to-command-execution flaw, CVSS 9.8) circulated this week, but the primary write-up was inaccessible and we could not confirm it against NVD or an MSRC advisory. `REVIEW:` tracking only — do not cite to clients until verified against the CVE record.
